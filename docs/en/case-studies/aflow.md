# ICLR 2025: AFlow writing analysis

## Paper context

- **Venue**: ICLR 2025
  ([Proceedings link](https://proceedings.iclr.cc/paper_files/paper/2025/file/5492ecbce4439401798dcd2c90be94cd-Paper-Conference.pdf))
- **Title**: AFlow: Automating Agentic Workflow Generation
- **Problem addressed**: Manual design of LLM agentic workflows
  (structured sequences of LLM invocations with detailed instructions)
  is a bottleneck for scalable adaptation of LLMs to new domains. Prior
  work has automated parts of this pipeline but not the full process;
  even code-based representations (ADAS) suffer from inefficient linear
  heuristic search.
- **Core contribution**: A Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)-based
  framework that represents workflows as code-based graphs (nodes,
  operators, edges), automatically explores the workflow search space,
  and discovers effective workflows with minimal human intervention
  across six benchmarks.

AFlow is a Technique Paper: it proposes a new method (MCTS over code-
represented agentic workflows) for a known problem (workflow
automation). The narrative axis is Key Idea or Mechanism.

## Writing craft: what works and why

### Introduction structure

AFlow's Introduction opens with the broad background (LLMs as powerful
tools for complex tasks), then quickly pivots to the core tension: the
rapid advancement of LLM capability is bottlenecked by manually
designed agentic workflows that do not scale. This is a well-executed
funnel: from general to specific in three sentences.

**Paragraph 1 (Background).** Opens with LLM capability claims in
several domains (code generation, data analysis, decision-making,
question answering), establishing that the stakes are real and the
audience is broad. The pivot sentence ("However, the rapid advancement
of LLMs heavily relies on manually designed agentic workflows...") is
the paper's central tension, exposed in the first paragraph.

**Paragraph 2 (Limitations).** Two limitations, each tied to a concrete
prior work:

- *Manual-setup-required automation.* Recent efforts have reduced human
  intervention but have not achieved full automation. Specific papers
  cited (Khattab et al. 2024; Yüksekgönül et al. 2024; Zhuge et al.
  2024) still require manual workflow setup, or have optimisation
  objectives too narrow to capture the full diversity of workflows.
- *Inefficient search even in code-based methods.* ADAS (Hu et al. 2024)
  represents workflows as code, which is expressive, but its linear
  heuristic search cannot find effective workflows in a reasonable
  number of iterations.

The two-limitation structure clearly positions AFlow: it is responding
to the "incomplete automation plus insufficient search efficiency"
axis, not to unrelated workflow-automation concerns.

**Paragraph 3 (Problem essence and Key Idea).** The Key Idea is
introduced as the transformation of workflow design into a code-level
search optimisation problem solved by MCTS. This is the inflection
point: the paper announces that the entire workflow space can be
explored algorithmically rather than crafted by hand.

**Paragraph 4 (Challenges).** Two implicit challenges, one enabling the
other: designing a search space expressive enough to cover workflow
variety, and navigating the huge search space efficiently. The first is
a representation challenge; the second is a search-algorithm challenge.
The two are orthogonal, which justifies the two technical points in
the Methodology.

**Paragraph 5 (Solution Overview).** Three technical points, each
explicitly addressing one aspect of the challenges:

1. *Workflow representation.* Workflows as graphs of nodes connected
   by code-based edges encoding logic, conditions, and dependencies.
2. *Operators.* Predefined reusable node combinations (for example,
   Ensemble, Review & Revise) that encode common agentic operations and
   serve as building blocks.
3. *MCTS search.* Soft mixed-probability selection, LLM-driven node
   expansion, execution-based evaluation, and experience
   backpropagation, together forming the search loop.

The third technical point (MCTS) is positioned as solving both "search
space too large" and "search quality too low" simultaneously, a one-
stone-two-birds argument that strengthens the Methodology's coherence.

**Paragraph 6 (Contributions).** Three explicit contributions:

1. Problem Formulation. Formalise workflow optimisation; unify prior
   approaches as specific cases.
2. AFlow. The MCTS-based method that discovers effective workflows
   across multiple domains.
3. Extensive Evaluation. Six benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP, MATH, GSM8K,
   HotPotQA, DROP); AFlow outperforms manual methods by 5.7% and
   existing automated methods by 19.5%; workflows generated by AFlow
   enable smaller LLMs to outperform larger models.

The contribution structure here differs from Alpha-SQL. AFlow's first
contribution is Problem Formulation, not a technique: this is partly a
Technique Paper and partly a formalisation contribution. A reviewer
sees the paper unifying prior methods under one framework as well as
proposing a new one.

### Methodology framing

![AFlow solution overview figure](/assets/images/aflow_solution_overview.png)

Figure: AFlow's Solution Overview (ICLR 2025), demonstrating the
Pipeline paradigm from [figure-designer](../skills/figure-designer/overview.md).
Three panels, left to right: Search Space, Search via AFlow, Search
Result.

The Solution Overview figure is a textbook Pipeline: Search Space on
the left (showing Node, Operator, Edge as code representations), Search
via AFlow in the middle (showing the MCTS four-step loop of Selection,
Expansion, Evaluation, Backpropagation), Search Result on the right
(showing the optimal workflows discovered for different task types). A
reader's eye travels naturally left to right, and every module label
matches a methodology subsection.

The methodology section itself mirrors the figure's left-to-right
structure. First, the search space is defined (nodes, operators,
edges); then the search algorithm is detailed (MCTS with the four-step
loop); then the search output is characterised (discovered workflows
and their properties). The section is self-contained: a reader could
understand AFlow from Figure 3 plus the methodology section alone,
without revisiting the Introduction.

### Figure design

Figure 2 (Motivated Example) uses the Existing vs Ours paradigm:
existing workflow representations (manual, partially-automated, code-
based) on the left, AFlow's graph representation (nodes connected by
code-edges) on the right. The visual contrast immediately conveys why
the new representation is more expressive.

Figure 3 (Solution Overview) uses the Pipeline paradigm as described
above.

Experimental figures follow the universal rules: saturated colour for
AFlow's results, muted colours for baselines; ColorBrewer palette;
honest axis ranges; self-contained captions that state findings in the
first sentence.

### Contribution phrasing

Three contributions, each specific and mapped to a section:

- Contribution 1 (Problem Formulation) maps to Section 3 (Methodology,
  specifically the Problem Formulation subsection).
- Contribution 2 (AFlow method) maps to Section 3 (Methodology,
  specifically the MCTS search algorithm subsection) and Section 4
  (implementation details).
- Contribution 3 (Extensive Evaluation) maps to Section 5 (Experiments).

No contribution is vague. The claim that "workflows generated by AFlow
enable smaller LLMs to outperform larger models" is a specific,
evidence-backed cost-performance argument with real implications for
downstream practitioners.

## Key techniques used

- **Funnel-style Background paragraph**: from broad LLM capability to
  the specific bottleneck (manual workflow design) in three sentences.
- **Two limitations tied to named prior work**: Khattab et al. 2024 and
  ADAS (Hu et al. 2024) are cited directly.
- **Key Idea as inflection point**: the reframing of workflow design
  into code-level search optimisation is announced explicitly,
  signalling the paper's main reframing.
- **Two challenges covering representation and search**, orthogonal by
  design; this lets the Methodology have three technical points that do
  not overlap.
- **One-stone-two-birds argument for MCTS**: solves both "search too
  large" and "search quality too low" simultaneously.
- **Pipeline figure mirroring methodology structure**: left to right in
  the figure corresponds to earlier to later in the text, making
  navigation trivial.
- **Problem Formulation as a contribution**: explicit unification of
  prior methods under the new framework, which strengthens the paper's
  positioning as foundational rather than incremental.
- **Cost-performance result as a downstream implication**: smaller LLMs
  with AFlow workflows beating larger LLMs is an applied-value claim
  beyond accuracy numbers.

## Lessons for your paper

**Consider making Problem Formulation one of your contributions when
your framework unifies prior work.** If the reframing you propose
subsumes several prior methods as specific cases, this is itself a
contribution. Stating it explicitly in the Introduction's contributions
list positions the paper as foundational rather than incremental.

**Design orthogonal challenges.** When your challenges cover
independent axes (representation vs search, efficiency vs accuracy,
offline vs online), the methodology's technical points naturally map to
them without overlap. Overlapping challenges produce overlapping
methodology points, which reviewers read as padding.

**Use one-stone-two-birds arguments when genuine.** If one technical
choice solves two problems at once, say so explicitly. It is a strong
signal of coherence. But do not manufacture this: if MCTS solves
search-size only and not search-quality, do not claim both.

**Align the Solution Overview figure with the methodology section
order.** Left-to-right in the figure should equal earlier-to-later in
the text. A reader's eye trained by the figure should land on the
right text without effort. Misaligned figures force the reader to
translate, and translation is where comprehension fails.

**Applied-value claims strengthen experimental contributions.** Raw
accuracy numbers are necessary; downstream implications (smaller models
now competitive, lower inference cost, new deployment settings) are
memorable. If your experiments support such an implication, state it
in the contributions list.
